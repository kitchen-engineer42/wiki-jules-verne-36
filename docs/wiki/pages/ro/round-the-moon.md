## Overview

*Round the Moon* is Jules Verne's sequel to *From the Earth to the Moon*, carrying the tale from the launch into deep space. It opens as "Michel Ardan, Barbicane, and Nicholl said good-bye to the numerous friends they left upon the earth" and take their places in the projectile (RM-001-002).

Two dogs share the voyage, "destined to acclimatise the canine race upon the lunar continents" (RM-001-002).

## Plot Summary

Sealed in their padded bullet, the three await the firing: "at thirteen minutes to eleven Murchison will set fire to the Columbiad; at that minute precisely we shall leave our spheroid," says Barbicane (RM-001-013). Once the gas is lit, "the projectile presented the aspect of a comfortable room with padded walls, furnished with circular divans" (RM-001-007).

The travellers never reach the Moon's surface. Deflected from their course, "the projectile, kept up by inexplicable forces, has grazed the north pole of the satellite at less than twenty-five miles' distance" (RM-015-005). Instead they are "dragged round the moon," sweeping past its hidden face without landing (RM-015-040).

Swinging back toward Earth, the projectile falls into the sea: "In north lat. 20° 7', and west long. 41° 37', the projectile of the Columbiad fell into the Pacific, on December 12th, at 1.17 am" (RM-021-027). The corvette *Susquehanna* recovers the wanderers alive.

## Main Characters

- Barbicane — president of the Gun Club, commander of the lunar expedition
- Michel Ardan — the exuberant Frenchman who proposed to ride the projectile
- Captain Nicholl — Barbicane's methodical former rival, third of the travellers

## Key Places & Technology

- The projectile — the padded aluminium bullet that carries the three round the Moon
- The corvette *Susquehanna* — the ship that recovers the travellers from the Pacific

## Themes

Verne turns the second half of his lunar voyage into a chamber drama of science and temperament, as calculation, doubt, and gaiety pass the hours between worlds. The failure to land — the projectile merely circling the Moon — lets him survey the satellite while preserving the wonder of the unreachable.
