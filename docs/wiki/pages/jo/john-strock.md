# John Strock

## Overview

He is the story's narrator and a senior federal officer — the case, in the end, is filed "after the name of John Strock, chief inspector of the federal police in Washington" (MW-017-007).

His chief has long relied on him, asking whether he is still the man "who on so many occasions has proven to me both his devotion and his ability" (MW-002-003).

## Role in the Story

He is picked for the Great Eyrie inquiry personally: "I have made choice of an agent in whom we have full confidence; and this agent is you, Strock" (MW-002-025).

He is given a free hand — "You will be free to act according to circumstances" (MW-002-029) — and, when the Terror appears, "I have chosen you, Strock, to follow him the instant he appears" (MW-008-028), sealed with "I now give you a formal order" (MW-010-066).

His charge is explicit: he is the officer "to whom had been assigned the task of penetrating the Great Eyrie" (MW-013-042).

Even in the story's climax his duty defines him: "Was I or was I not Strock, chief inspector of the federal police" (MW-017-066).

## Character & Traits

He is driven by "the desire, which has always been very strong in me, to investigate and understand everything which is mysterious" (MW-001-002).

His superior rates him highly: "I regard you as specially fitted for the work" (MW-002-027).

Yet he knows failure too, greeted after his first fruitless ascent as "Our poor Strock, who has failed" (MW-005-006).

## Relationships

- Mr. Ward — his chief at the federal police, who directs his missions and, after the Eyrie, absolves him: "you accomplished everything that depended on human powers; and that no blame attaches to you" (MW-008-016).

- [[Robur]] — the inventor he is set to capture; after the storm he finds himself alone alive, "Robur the Conqueror and his two companions had ended their adventurous careers in the waters of the Gulf" (MW-018-004).

## Appearances

- [[The Master of the World]] — where he is drawn into the Terror's world and nearly drowns, saved only because "I should have been suffocated, if someone had not dragged me from the lake" (MW-013-004).

- During the final pursuit "I resolved to await the destroyers' closest approach and at the last moment I would decide" (MW-014-028); afterward the press declared "Thanks to Inspector Strock the American police still lead the world" (MW-018-016).
