## Overview

The Moon is the celestial destination that drives the two-volume lunar saga: the Gun Club fires a manned projectile at it in *From the Earth to the Moon* and observes it at close range in *Round the Moon*. The whole scheme turns on the geometry of the shot — the projectile must be "hurled so as to reach the moon at a particular point" (FEM-004-011), timed to the satellite's orbit.

Once the travelers reach its vicinity, the Moon ceases to be an abstract target and becomes a landscape studied in detail. *Round the Moon* devotes long passages to its surface, treating the lunar disc as a world with its own geography.

## In the Narrative

As the projectile skirts the Moon, the voyagers survey its face like explorers charting a new continent. They note that its landmasses are unevenly distributed: on the lunar spheroid "the continents occupy the more southern hemisphere" (RM-011-005), an asymmetry unlike Earth's.

They also catalogue its broken, watery-seeming terrain. "Islands are numerous on the surface of the moon" (RM-011-007), the observers record, mapping the shapes that earlier astronomers had taken for seas and archipelagoes.

## Geography & Features

The Moon is rendered as a rugged, volcanic, airless world. Its surface is "like an immense Switzerland — a continual Norway" (RM-011-008), so profoundly mountainous that the lunar disc is prized as a natural laboratory for the study of great geological phenomena.

Its most conspicuous formations are craters. Copernicus "forms the most important radiating system" of the visible hemisphere (RM-012-018), and features like Tycho are read as extinct volcanoes, "only an extinct volcano, like those on that side of the moon" (RM-012-019). Above all the Moon is a dead world without air: "the moon having no atmosphere" (RM-013-043), a fact that shapes every inference the travelers draw about its lifelessness.

## Connections
