# Yaquita

## Overview

She grew up mistress of the Iquitos fazenda: "his daughter Yaquita, who after the death of her mother had taken charge" (EHLA-003-010) of her father Magalhaes' house.

She married young — "Yaquita was then twenty-two years old, Joam was twenty-six" (EHLA-003-026).

## Role in the Story

Her father, dying, gave her to Joam, for "he raised up Yaquita, who was weeping by his side, took her hand, and put it into that of Joam Garral" (EHLA-003-022), and "A year after her marriage Yaquita presented her husband with a son, and, two years after, a daughter" (EHLA-003-029).

It is she who moves the family to descend the river, though "to persuade her husband to leave the fazenda Yaquita felt to be a very serious matter" (EHLA-004-014); and as matriarch aboard the raft she plays hostess, when "Yaquita invited the commandant and his wife to come and have theirs on board the jangada" (EHLA-012-044).

## Character & Traits

Her tenderness is the comfort of her husband's life — she speaks "with that gentle voice which had been to him all the music of his life" (EHLA-004-021).

She is devoted and courageous, "the devoted wife and brave companion he had ever known her to be" (EHLA-026-003), and holds duty sacred: "It was her duty to her husband, Manoel" (EHLA-011-065).

## Relationships

- [[Joam Garral]] — her husband, in whose innocence and mercy he trusts absolutely, certain that "Yaquita will pardon everything" (EHLA-023-014); at the end "Joam Dacosta clasped Yaquita to his heart" (EHLA-039-004).

- [[Benito Garral]] and [[Minha Garral]] — her children, who share her long vigil, for it was "the hope of Yaquita, of Benito, of Manoel, and of Minha" (EHLA-032-018) to save Joam.

- [[Manoel Valdez]] — her daughter's betrothed, whom she blesses, "may you be as happy as Minha and Manoel, and as I and your father have been" (EHLA-018-053).

## Appearances

- [[Eight Hundred Leagues on the Amazon]] — where a shadow falls as "Yaquita and her daughter silently followed, and all felt an unaccountable impression of sadness, as if they had a presentiment of some coming calamity" (EHLA-019-048).

- Through the imprisonment she keeps "the few hours which each day the husband and wife spent together" (EHLA-037-001), and the tale closes as "the same bands which had formerly blessed Joam and Yaquita were again stretched forth" (EHLA-040-040) over their children's wedding.
