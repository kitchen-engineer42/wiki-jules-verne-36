# Dame Hansen

## Overview

She keeps the well-known hostelry of the Telemark — "In a word, it is Dame Hansen's inn" (TN-002-008).

Ole Kamp pictures her in his letter home, "sitting in her wooden arm-chair by the old stove in the big hall" (TN-001-085).

## Role in the Story

Her troubles spring from ruinous ventures, for "Ever since her husband's death, Dame Hansen has been engaging in unsuccessful speculation" (TN-014-095), leaving "a receipt for the amount of the mortgage on the Dal property" (TN-014-113) in the usurer's hands.

When the debt falls due "Dame Hansen had used it to free herself" (TN-015-007) — sacrificing the very lottery ticket her daughter had given her — and throughout she opposes the family's hopes, so that Hulda's loyalty to Ole "excited the bitter disapproval of Dame Hansen" (TN-014-005).

## Character & Traits

She is grave and secretive; through the tale "Dame Hansen seemed to have become even more gloomy and anxious and reticent than ever" (TN-004-065), answering even her own children coldly — she "inquired Dame Hansen, coldly" (TN-005-031).

At heart she is superstitious, "this Norwegian, being rather superstitious in her secret heart" (TN-015-037).

## Relationships

- [[Hulda Hansen]] — her devoted daughter, whose gift she takes, since "In giving the ticket to Dame Hansen, Hulda had been prompted by a filial sentiment" (TN-015-015).

- [[Joel Hansen]] — her son, who tries to comfort her in her shame; "cried Joel, approaching Dame Hansen, and endeavoring to remove her hands from her face" (TN-014-100).

- [[Sylvius Hogg]] — the guest who cannot draw out her secret, for "she was so uncommunicative that he was obliged to abandon all hope" (TN-011-067).

- Sandgoist — the usurer she dreads, and the story turns on "Dame Hansen's agitation in his presence" (TN-007-002).

## Appearances

- [[Ticket No. 9672]] — at the fatal reckoning "Dame Hansen seized it, and as she exchanged it for Sandgoist's receipt" (TN-014-120) her daughter nearly faints.

- She first eyes the usurer warily, when "Dame Hansen and her daughter were watching him from one of the windows of the inn" (TN-006-047), and at the joyful end "even Dame Hansen, who was no longer haunted by a fear of Sandgoist" (TN-020-025) shares the family's peace.
