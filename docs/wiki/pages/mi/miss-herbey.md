# Miss Herbey

## Overview

She appears in the passenger list as "Miss Herbey, a young English lady, companion to Mrs. Kear" (SC2-002-006).

She is "a young English girl of about twenty" (SC2-004-018), in the paid service of the querulous Mrs. Kear.

After her mistress dies she is left with nothing: "She had been, she said, two years with Mrs. Kear, and although now left alone in the world, homeless and without resources, hope for the future does not fail her" (SC2-032-011).

## Role in the Story

She nurses her mistress with a devotion the narrator admires — "unrelaxing in her attentions, and the unremitted diligence with which she fulfills her offices of duty" (SC2-012-015).

When fire reaches the cabins she saves her mistress, who "shrieking wildly, is brought on deck by Miss Herbey" (SC2-013-017).

Starving herself for others, "Miss Herbey reserved almost the whole of her own share for his use" (SC2-033-008).

She helps bury the first of the dead, for "after Miss Herbey and I had carefully wrapped the garments about it, with a few short prayers the body of the first victim of our miseries was committed to the deep" (SC2-028-008).

At the last she resolves on a life of service: "Miss Herbey by chance intimated to us her intention of retiring from the world and devoting the remainder of her life to the care of the sick and suffering" (SC2-057-014).

## Character & Traits

Even in servitude "she never utters a word of open complaint, but quietly and gracefully performs her duties accepting without a murmur the paltry salary" (SC2-004-019).

She meets the horror with "a calm Christian fortitude, which was shown by no one else of the party except Miss Herbey, who faced her danger with the same brave composure" (SC2-028-005).

Asked whether she fears death, she answers that she fears "only for those to whom life is precious" (SC2-027-013).

When the death-lots are drawn and her own name comes up, "the young girl heard it without a start" (SC2-053-008).

## Relationships

- Mrs. Kear, her capricious mistress, allows her no liberty: whenever Miss Herbey seeks the others' company, "she had barely reached the poop, when Mrs. Kear's languid voice was heard recalling her" (SC2-005-019).

- [[Robert Curtis]] — the survivors' leader, who joins the others in heeding her plea for mercy: "Curtis and Falsten agreed with me, and we all united to support Miss Herbey's merciful petition" (SC2-054-006).

- The Letourneurs take her in at the last, for "Miss Herbey, after some deliberation, consented to become a member of their family, and finds in M. Letourneur a father, and in Andre a brother" (SC2-057-016).

## Appearances

- [[The Survivors of the Chancellor]] — at whose rescue "Miss Herbey's voice was heard pouring out fervent praise to Heaven, and we were all glad to unite our thanksgivings with hers" (SC2-057-002).
