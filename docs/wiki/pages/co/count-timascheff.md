# Count Timascheff

## Overview

He is a grandee of means — "Count Timascheff, a Russian nobleman, was evidently rich" (OC-040-027).

It was as "the owner of the _Dobryna_ to winter in these parts" (OC-002-019) that his yacht lay off the Algerian coast when the catastrophe struck.

## Role in the Story

He places every resource at the captain's service — "Count Timascheff, anxious that Servadac should have every facility afforded him for solving his doubts" (OC-011-020) supports the survey of the new sea.

At Gibraltar, affronted by the English officers, "having poured out a torrent of angry invective against the English officers, he ordered his crew to embark immediately" (OC-015-080); and later, to relieve the colony, "Count Timascheff, expressing his regret that his purse contained only paper money, begged to place it at Captain Servadac's disposal" (OC-032-066).

## Character & Traits

He is unfailingly courteous: "With his usual air of stately courtesy, Count Timascheff begged the captain to consider the yacht at his own disposal" (OC-013-007).

He is measured and self-possessed, for "Although Count Timascheff secretly sympathized with Servadac, he had carefully refrained from taking part in the dispute" (OC-015-073).

He is devout and reflective, holding that "we are at the disposal of an over-ruling Providence" (OC-042-007) and observing wryly that one use of learning "is to make us know our own ignorance" (OC-042-014).

## Relationships

- [[Hector Servadac]] — first his rival, "amongst these not the least formidable was the Russian Count Timascheff" (OC-003-015), later his fast friend, when "Count Timascheff laid his hand kindly on the captain's shoulder" (OC-017-030).

- [[Lieutenant Procope]] — his trusted officer, on whom he relies, "leaving the command of his yacht in the hands of Lieutenant Procope" (OC-011-001).

- [[Isaac Hakkabut]] — the trader whose greed he marvels at, asking "But how in the world can you ever make those fellows pay anything" (OC-019-059).

## Appearances

- [[Off on a Comet]] — after the shock "Count Timascheff had landed on the island" (OC-010-010) to rejoin Servadac, and at the parting of the sledge party "Count Timascheff could not forbear pressing his two brave friends to his bosom" (OC-025-027).

- As the climax nears, "the less Hector Servadac and Count Timascheff had to say to each other" (OC-043-048), each absorbed in the peril to come.
