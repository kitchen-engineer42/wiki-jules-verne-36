# Crockston

## Overview

He signs on for the daring voyage, when "the sailor Crockston and his nephew were inscribed in the muster-roll of the Dolphin" (BR-002-049).

He is above all a loyal retainer of the Halliburtts, Jenny herself owning she had "no help but that of Crockston, our faithful servant" (BR-003-067).

## Role in the Story

Once aboard he opens Jenny's cause, so that soon "Miss Halliburtt's story, which Crockston was no longer hindered from telling" (BR-004-002) is known to all the crew.

He is the schemer of the rescue, urging "we must use stronger measures, and carry off the prisoner by force" (BR-004-031), and to reach the shore he has himself feigned a captive — "Crockston, with his feet and hands in irons, was taken on shore" (BR-008-058) — so that in the end "The man who has saved you and brought you here was your servant Crockston" (BR-008-112).

## Character & Traits

He is utterly intrepid, "the American, who feared nothing" (BR-007-044), and his daring astonishes the captain, who is "amazed at Crockston's boldness" (BR-004-020).

Even James Playfair salutes his spirit: "I understand, my good fellow, you have got some pluck" (BR-007-048).

## Relationships

- [[Jenny Halliburtt]] — his young mistress, whom he serves body and soul; she cries "Dear Crockston, you are going to risk your life then, to save my father" (BR-008-038).

- [[James Playfair]] — the captain who comes to trust him, sending him off with "I understand you, my friend; and I only regret one thing" (BR-008-026).

- Mr. Halliburtt — Jenny's imprisoned father, whom he brings out; on the escape "Crockston sat at the bows, and Mr. Halliburtt at the stern" (BR-009-002).

## Appearances

- [[The Blockade Runners]] — where he is first hustled off to enlist, for "Crockston did not want to be told twice" (BR-002-046), then endures the boatswain's lash when "two strong sailors stripped Crockston of his woollen jersey" (BR-003-048).

- In the escape he saves the ship, for "It was Crockston; he took the shell in his strong arms" (BR-009-073) and flung it overboard, and at the wedding stands "the worthy Crockston, resplendent in apple-green clothes, with gold buttons" (BR-010-005).
